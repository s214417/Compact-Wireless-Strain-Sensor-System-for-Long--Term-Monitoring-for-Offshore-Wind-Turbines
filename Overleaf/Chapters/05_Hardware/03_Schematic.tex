\subsection{Microcontroller Selection}
The microcontroller (MCU) used in this implementation is Silicon labs' EFR32MG22E, which is the optimal choice for this design due to its low-power design, versatility, and its low price. The MCU has a 32-bit ARM® Cortex®-M33 core with 76.8 MHz maximum operating frequency and has a suitable low amount of storage with up to 512 kB of flash and 32 kB of RAM, since it does not need to store many readings before it transmits data onwards. 

The MCU also features an seperate ARM Cortex M0+ Radio Controller with up to 6 dBm (2.4 GHz) TX output power, making it useful when working with zigbee connections. A TX power of 6 dBm is acceptable for this implementation, but could prove to be not enough in the final implementation, since a windmill flanger is made of solid metal and therefore could compromise the radio signal. Another solution could be to add extra routers if this happens or choose an MCU which can operate in the sub-GHz bands.
The MCU has an operating range between 1.71 V to 3.8 V single power supply, between a temperature of -40 °C to 125 °C. It has a power profile of 8.2 mA TX current at 6 dBm output power and min 0.17 $\upmu$A current in the lowest power mode EM4. The build in IADC has a resolution of 12-bit at 1 Msps and of 16-bit at 76.9 ksps and has integrated voltage reference options, which makes it possible to optimize the ADC input range and precision.

Compared to other suitable microcontrollers the EFR32MG22E has the lowest sleep current and lowest TX current at 6 dBm. It is also among the cheapest options at 14.32 DKK pr. unit, and eventhough it is a more expensive than the standard EFR32MG22, it is preferrable, because of its faster stardup times at 1.83 ms compared to 8.81 ms, from lowest standby mode EM4. That is 4.8 times faster than the EFR32MG22, which makes for a power saving of 79.2\% in startup, when going from EM4, which is the lowest power mode, to EM0, that is normal run mode.

\begin{equation}
    \text{Savings(\%)} = \frac{t_{normal}-t_{fast}}{t_{normal}} \cdot 100 = \frac{8.81 \text{ms} - 1.83 \text{ms}}{8.81 \text{ms}} \cdot 100 = 79.2 \%
\end{equation}

\subsection{Power Section}
The design of the power section is build upon Silicon Labs' guide 'AN0002.2: EFM32 and EFR32 Wireless Gecko Series 2 Hardware Design Considerations' \cite{Hardware_design_considerations} and the schematic of Silicons Labs' development kit build around the EFR32MG22E  \cite{Dev_Kit_Schematic}. \newline
The MCU has an operating range between 1.71 V to 3.8 V single power supply. To optimize the voltage range on the ADC readings the MCU will be driven with 3 V. The MCU has a build in DCDC-converter, which makes it possible to generate an input voltage of 1.8 V for peripherals that do not need a 3 V input. This includes PAVDD (Supply to radio power amplifier), RFVDD (Supply to radio analog and HFXO) and IOVDD (I/O power supply). These inputs do not need 3 V since  it is transmitting with 6 dBm output power on Tx, and a higher input voltage is only needed for 14 dBm and above, and there are no GPIO outputs that need 3 V. VREGSW (DC-DC powertrain switching node) is connected to DVDD (Input to the internal digital LDO) via an inductor L4 which helps with energy storage and smoothing during switching together with a capacitor C9 which filters voltage ripple. 

Decoupling capacitors C3, C4, C7, C8, C9, C14 are based on Silicon Labs' recommended values. In Silicon labs' application notes it is mentioned that decoupling should exist of one large and one small capacitor to mitigate both high and low frequency nosie. In this design it has been determined that a single larger decoupling capacitor is sufficiton, since smd capacitors are short leaded, and that high frequency impedance is not about the capacitance but the lead inductance, therefore a single short leaded smd bulk capacitor works fine for decoupling \cite{}. All decoupling capacitors have been chosen to be ceramic X5R or X7R since the system will be operating in a wide temperature range, and they have a more staedy capacitance across a wider temperature range.

Ferrite beads FB1, FB2 and FB3 have been added as an extra precaution to mitigate high frequency noise as much as possible. The system could work without them, but it could make for a more precise ADC measurement, which is preferable.

A reset button SW1 with fitting debounce has also been integrated to make for an easier debugging. The debounce section consists of resistor R3, working as a pull-down resistor, and capacitor C15 which helps to smooths switch bounce and noise.

\subsection{Battery Selection}
For this design, the chosen primary cell is the EVE ER14250 Lithium Thionyl Chloride (Li-SOCl₂) battery, size ½AA, with a nominal capacity of 1200 mAh. This battery has a high energy density and operates over a wide temperature range from \SI{-60}{\celsius} to \SI{+80}{\celsius} \cite{Battery}. It provides a pulse current capacity of up to 50 mA, enabling it to reliably supply the higher current pulses required during radio transmission (Tx) and reception (Rx) events. In addition, its self-discharge rate is exceptionally low at only 1\% per year, which is essential for achieving a 10+ year operational lifetime in ultra-low-power applications.

Although Li-SOCl₂ batteries are more expensive than conventional coin cells, they are a keystone in making this product viable, given the requirements for both long lifetime and reliable pulse performance under varying environmental conditions. As of this writing, the most economical EVE ER14250 battery was found on the European battery distributor nkon.nl at a price of 1.45 $\texteuro$ (approximately 10.83 DKK)\cite{BatteryPrice}.
For comparison, the most economical appropriate CR2477 1000 mAh coin cell battery was found at the Danish distributor Batteribyen.dk for approximately 6,00 DKK per unit. To enable reliable operation for pulsed loads, a bulk capacitor is required due to the high internal resistance typical of coin cells. The cost for such a capacitor is approximately 1.60 DKK per unit from Digikey.com \cite{Bulk_cap}, resulting in a total cost of 7.60 DKK per unit for the coin cell solution. This is 3.23 DKK less per unit than the Li-SOCl₂ solution. Despite this lower initial cost, the Li-SOCl₂ battery offers significant advantages and is more probable to last 10+ years, due to a wider operational temperature range and a lower self-discharge rate.

\subsection{External Crystal Oscillators}
Two external crystal oscillators X1 and X2 have been added to the circuit eventhough the MCU already has four integrated clocks. The integrated clocks are RC oscillators and both resistance and capacitance varies with temperature more than the properties of quartz. Therefore it is favourable to add two external crystal oscillators to optimize the precision of the clocks over a wider range of temperatures.

\subsection{PMOS and Sensor Readings}
To reduce current consumption a PMOS transitor \cite{PMOS} is used to switch on the power to the sensors by connecting them to the 3 V VMCU voltage from the LDO. The PMOS is selected based on that it has ultra low shutdown leakage current of 0.20 nA and a low quiescent current of 0.01 nA to mitigate power waste. The PMOS is being enabled by GPIO pin PC00 which pulls it high during data collection. The enable network also has a pull-down resistor to avoid current spikes during reset of the MCU. Only powering the sensors during data collection will minimize leakage current from the sensors and reduce overall power consumption.

\subsection{RF section and impedance matching}
The design of the RF section is based on Silicon Labs' 'AN930.2: EFR32 Series 2 2.4 GHz
Matching Guide' \cite{Matching_guide} and 'UG582: EFR32xG22E Explorer Kit User's Guide' \cite{User_guide}. Silicon labs recommend using a T-filter for layouts using the EFR32MG22E chip, since it is less sensetive to layout variations compared to a PI-filter and achieves the best radiated harmonic margin for boards with a distance between the top layer and the first GND layer smaller than 800 $\upmu$m. Due to bonding wires and parasitics, Silicon Labs recommend a optimum termination impedance of $37 + j5\, \Omega$ at the pin RF2G4\_IO. For ease of design, the impedance matching circuit chosen for this design is highly inspired by the design Silicon Labs uses for their explorer kit with the same 2.4 GHz 50 $\Omega$ antenna \cite{Antenna}. Equations \eqref{eq:ZL1}--\eqref{eq:Ztotal} are used to calculate impedance matching at the pin for this design. 
\begin{align}
Z_{L1} &= j\omega L_1 = j\,40.72\,\Omega \label{eq:ZL1}\\
Z_{L2} &= j\omega L_2 = j\,45.24\,\Omega \label{eq:ZL2}\\
Z_{L3} &= j\omega L_3 = j\,241.27\,\Omega \label{eq:ZL3}\\
Z_{C1} &= \frac{1}{j\omega C_1} = -j\,3.68\,\Omega \label{eq:ZC1}\\
Z_{C2} &= \frac{1}{j\omega C_2} = -j\,41.45\,\Omega \label{eq:ZC2}\\
Z_{C5} &= \frac{1}{j\omega C_5} = -j\,663.15\,\Omega \label{eq:ZC5}\\
Z_{C6} &= \frac{1}{j\omega C_6} = -j\,55.26\,\Omega \label{eq:ZC6}\\
Z_{\text{ant}} &= 50\,\Omega \label{eq:Zant}
\end{align}

\begin{align}
Z_a &= Z_{\text{ant}} \parallel Z_{L3} = \frac{1}{\dfrac{1}{50\,\Omega} + \dfrac{1}{j\,241.27\,\Omega}} = 47.94 + j\,9.93\,\Omega \label{eq:Za}\\[0.5em]
Z_b &= Z_a + Z_{L2} + Z_{C1} + Z_{C2} \notag\\
&= 47.94 + j\,9.93 + j\,45.24 - j\,3.68 - j\,41.45 = 47.94 + j\,10.04\,\Omega \label{eq:Zb}\\[0.5em]
Z_c &= Z_b \parallel Z_{C6} = \frac{1}{\dfrac{1}{47.94 + j\,10.04\,\Omega} + \dfrac{1}{-j\,55.26\,\Omega}} = 33.71 - j\,23.47\,\Omega \label{eq:Zc}\\[0.5em]
Z_d &= Z_c + Z_{L1} = 33.71 - j\,23.47 + j\,40.72 = 33.71 + j\,17.25\,\Omega \label{eq:Zd}\\[0.5em]
Z_{total} &= Z_d \parallel Z_{C5} = \frac{1}{\dfrac{1}{33.71 + j\,17.25\,\Omega} + \dfrac{1}{-j\,663.15\,\Omega}} = 35.44 + j\,15.86\,\Omega \label{eq:Ztotal}
\end{align}

From equation \eqref{eq:Ztotal} the impedance is calculated to be $35.44 + j\,15.86\,\Omega$ at the pin, which is close to Silicon Labs' theoretical recommendation, but since their explorer board's desing was tested further taken into account other parasitics from variations in boards thickness and trace lengths, it is only appropriate to closely replicate the layout of the explorer kit to get the most effective RF capabilities.

\subsection{Connectors}
The connectors selected for this design are JST‑SH board headers, chosen for their compact footprint. Three connectors are used: J1, a 2‑pin connector that connects the battery and provides the input supply voltage; J2, a 4‑pin connector that supplies the sensor interface and carries the sensor measurement signals; and P2, a 6‑pin debug connector that exposes the serial wire debug (SWD) programming signals (SWCLK, SWDIO, SWO, nRESET) as well as VDCDC and GND.

\subsection{Final Schematic Design}
\label{Final_Schem}
After assembling and testing the PCB, some alterations were made to the schematic. 

\textbf{LDO}\newline
The LDO was removed since a stable voltage of 3 V is not needed, since the precision of the sensor readings does not depend on the voltage input in a wheatstone formation. AVDD was also changed to be powered from the DCDC-converter with 1.8 V, since the voltage on AVDD has to match the voltage on IOVDD. When IOVDD was powered from 1.8 V and AVDD from 3 V, it was noticed that the voltage range was set to the lower of the two, making it obsolete to power AVDD at 3 V via an LDO.

\textbf{PMOS}\newline
The PMOS was used to power the sensors with the batteries 3 V input, but since the voltage range has been lowered to 1.8 V due to the rewiring of AVDD, the sensors can be powered from a GPIO driven high at 1.8 V, which removes the need of a PMOS.

\textbf{Ferrite Beads}\newline
When measuring the voltage on VDCDC the noise with and without the ferrite beads was equal and they made no difference in the implementation. This is mostly due to the battery having a low noise and to L4 effeciently reducing switching noise from the DCDC-converter.

\textbf{Reset Circuit}\newline
The reset circuit was removed in the final implementation, since a reset button is mostly preffered during debugging, but is not necessary in the final implementation.

\textbf{LFXO Low Frequency Clock}\newline
The low frequency clock X2 was removed due to it not being used in the final implementation, since it is more relevant for low power modes EM1-EM3, \autoref{Sec:Power_Modes}. For an applicaiton that only runs in EM0 and EM4 it is not necessary, since the external high frequency clock HFXO X1 is used in EM0 and the integrated ultra-low frequency 1 kHz RC oscillator ULFRCO is used in EM4.
