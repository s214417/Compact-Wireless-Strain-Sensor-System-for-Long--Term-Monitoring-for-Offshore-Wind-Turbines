\section{Strain Sensing Performance}\label{sec:strain_sensing_performance}

This section evaluates the assembled half-bridge as a complete strain-sensing element, covering its response to a strain ramp, its hysteresis under repeated loading, its stability at rest, and its behavior over multiple cycles and under varying temperature. The results are drawn from more than one physical bridge assembly, where a closely matched bridge illustrates the best-case response, hysteresis, and rest stability, while a second bridge that came out of the same print process with a pronounced arm mismatch, as a consequence of the print-to-print variability rather than by design, is used for the long-term repeatability tests.

The response of the closely matched half-bridge to a controlled strain profile is shown in \cref{fig:Wheatstone2}, where the upper trace is the commanded strain and the lower trace is the bridge output normalized to the \SI{1.8}{\volt} excitation and expressed in \si{\milli\volt\per\volt}. The profile comprises an initial fast excursion that exercises the full strain range, followed by a slow symmetric ramp up to \SI{800}{\micro\strain} and back. At the \SI{1.8}{\volt} excitation the bridge output ranges from approximately \SI{-188}{\milli\volt} to \SI{206}{\milli\volt} across the profile, equivalent to about \SI{-104}{\milli\volt\per\volt} and \SI{114}{\milli\volt\per\volt} on the normalized axis, giving a full-scale swing of close to \SI{0.4}{\volt}. The output increases monotonically with the applied strain and reverses cleanly on unloading, returning close to its starting level, and the swing is large compared with the millivolt-level output of a conventional foil-gauge bridge. This bridge was printed with the same parameters as the example characterized in \Cref{Wheatstone Half-Bridge Design}, yet it balances and tracks more cleanly, a difference attributable to the print-to-print and test-setup variability discussed in \Cref{sec:Discussion}.

A sawtooth strain signal of progressively decreasing amplitude was applied by the motorized stage, and the commanded strain together with the resulting bridge output is shown in the \cref{fig:Sawtooth_baseline,fig:Sawtooth_center}, where successive cycles relax toward the unloaded start point and toward the mid-range point respectively. 
The peak commanded strain in both figures is \num{125} microsteps (\SI{24.41}{\micro\meter}) rather than \num{123} microsteps (\SI{24.02}{\micro\meter}), chosen so that the step count is divisible by five. The smallest commanded strain, reached in the final peak of \cref{fig:Sawtooth_baseline}, is accordingly \num{25} microsteps, corresponding to \SI{4.88}{\micro\meter} or a strain of \SI{0.016}{\percent}.
In \cref{fig:Sawtooth_center}, the peaks and valleys instead close in on the mid-range point in half-sized steps, so the smallest commanded strain is half of this value, \num{12.5} microsteps. Because the stage moves only in whole microsteps, this rounds up to \num{13} (\SI{2.54}{\micro\meter}).
The output follows the sawtooth strain closely across the whole run and continues to reproduce the commanded waveform even as the amplitude is reduced toward the smallest strains, where each cycle remains clearly resolved. Plotting the output against the applied strain in the lower plot of each figure reveals a hysteresis loop in which the loading and unloading branches do not coincide, with the output lagging the strain as the load is released. The loop becomes narrower as the cycle amplitude is reduced through the run, and relaxing the cycles toward the mid-range point holds the contacts away from the near-separation extreme, which narrows the loop further around the operating point. This hysteresis is consistent with viscoelastic relaxation in the substrate and the settling of the asperity contacts, the same mechanism that produced the loading lag observed on the active elements in \Cref{Wheatstone Half-Bridge Design}.

The stability of the balanced bridge under constant environmental conditions is shown in \cref{fig:Wheatstone_drift}, recorded over an extended period with no strain applied. A slight drift is present at the start of the measurement while the assembly settles, after which the output stabilizes and no appreciable drift is observed for the remainder of the test.

The behavior of the bridge under deliberately varying temperature, by contrast, is shown in \cref{fig:FS_ADCogVout}, where the full module is implemented using the assembled final PCB design described in \Cref{sec:final_pcb} to acquire data via ZHA. The test setup, including the linear stage actuator, stepper motor, stepper driver and Arduino UNO, was placed together with the bias sensors, battery and PCB in a climate chamber. The chamber was held at a constant relative humidity of \SI{30}{\percent} while the temperature was cycled over the range \SIrange{-20}{50}{\celsius}. The linear actuator was not activated during the test but was used to fasten the sensors to an optimum stretch to optimize the Wheatstone bridge stability. The results in \cref{fig:FS_ADCogVout} show the Wheatstone bridge output measured by both the Keithley 2450 and the on-board IADC.

\includestandalone[mode=buildmissing]{Standalone/Plots/FullSystem_neg_deg_ADCogVout}
    \caption{Output voltage \(V_{out}\) measured with the sourcemeter and with the on-board IADC, under varying environmental conditions.}

Across the thermal cycle the on-board IADC reproduces the bridge output measured by the Keithley 2450 closely, the two traces visibly following one another throughout the run. The mean deviation between them is approximately \SI{-1}{\milli\volt}, with the IADC reading marginally below \(V_{out}\). This value should be read as indicative of the agreement between the two acquisition paths rather than as an exact one-to-one calibration, since the \(V_{out}\) reference has been smoothed and post-processed to remove the samples acquired while the MCU was in its sleep phase between measurements, so the two signals are not a strict sample-for-sample comparison. The unprocessed IADC trace also retains more high-frequency noise than the conditioned reference.

Over the thermal cycle the bridge output varies substantially, spanning roughly \SIrange{15}{310}{\milli\volt} and exhibiting several pronounced transients. These excursions are not a simple function of temperature, as the output does not return to its earlier level when the chamber returns to a previously visited setpoint. At \SI{25}{\celsius} the output reads approximately \SI{20}{\milli\volt} at the start of the test but \SI{170}{\milli\volt} at the end, and the two \SI{50}{\celsius} plateaus differ by a similar margin, at approximately \SI{53}{\milli\volt} and \SI{83}{\milli\volt}. A fixed temperature dependence of the bridge, whether well compensated or not, would be reproducible against temperature and cannot account for this behavior. The excursions are instead more consistent with mechanical strain transmitted to the sensors as the test fixture expands and contracts over the imposed temperature range, which the bridge correctly registers as strain, than with a residual temperature sensitivity of the bridge itself. Because the sensors were not mechanically decoupled from this fixture-induced strain, its contribution cannot be separated from any temperature response of the bridge. This test can therefore neither confirm nor refute the temperature compensation of the Wheatstone bridge.

The long-term repeatability was assessed on a second bridge assembled from two markedly mismatched active elements, shown in \cref{fig:finalWheatstoneSensorer}, where active sensor~1 spans roughly three times the relative resistance change \(\Delta R/R_0\) of active sensor~2, approximately \num{0.6} against \num{0.2} over the \SI{800}{\micro\strain} range, in contrast to the closely matched pair of \cref{fig:Wheatstone_sensorer}. Because the half-bridge output is the difference of two independent voltage dividers, each arm contributes its swing separately and the maximum swing of \cref{eq:swing_max} generalizes to the sum of the two per-arm contributions, so that the achievable swing is set by the sum of the arm ranges rather than by their equality. The mismatch therefore does not suppress the signal, since the wider arm supplies most of the swing while the unequal baselines add only a constant offset that the bias sensors remove.

Consistent with this, the bridge output tracks the applied strain monotonically over the long slow-strain run of \cref{fig:Slope1}, recorded over approximately \SI{60}{\hour} under constant conditions of \SI{25}{\celsius} and \SI{30}{\percent} relative humidity in the climate chamber and measured simultaneously by the sourcemeter as \(V_{out}\) and by the on-board IADC as \(V_{ADC}\). The IADC reproduces the shape of the sourcemeter reference across every cycle, which confirms that the on-board acquisition path captures the bridge response faithfully. The two records differ only in vertical scale, where the IADC applies a gain of four, so that \(V_{ADC}\approx 4\,V_{out}\), which leaves the tracking unaffected.

While the bridge tracks strain throughout the run, the output is not perfectly repeatable from cycle to cycle, as quantified in \cref{fig:Slope2}. The normalized peak-to-valley swing grows by approximately \SI{6}{\percent} over the seventeen cycles, while the individual peak and valley levels shift over the same period, and the sourcemeter and IADC records agree closely throughout. Since the temperature and humidity were held constant for this run, the drift is attributed to continued mechanical settling and relaxation of the printed contacts rather than to environmental effects.

The sensitivity of the mismatched bridge to temperature is examined directly in \cref{fig:sidsteTest}, which repeats the slow strain cycling while the ambient temperature is varied between \SI{20}{\celsius} and \SI{25}{\celsius}. The envelope of the output, formed by the per-cycle peaks and valleys, follows the ambient temperature rather than remaining constant, confirming that a temperature-dependent component remains in the measurement. This residual sensitivity follows from the mismatch, since the temperature compensation of \cref{eq:bridge_comp} assumes that the differenced elements share the same relative temperature response, whereas elements resting at different operating points carry different temperature coefficients, so that the common-mode term is no longer fully rejected. The residual component motivates the firmware temperature compensation using the on-die sensor proposed in \cref{sec:Discussion}, the precision of which is established in \cref{sec:temp_sensor}.

The sensitivity of the sensing elements is summarized by the gauge factor, defined as the relative resistance change per unit strain,

\begin{align}\label{eq:gauge_factor}
    \mathit{GF} = \frac{\Delta R / R_0}{\varepsilon}.
\end{align}

Within the small-strain operating range of this application the response is approximately linear, as seen for the optimized conductive-PLA element in \cref{fig:opt_pla}, so the gauge factor is effectively constant over this range and can be read directly as the slope of \(\Delta R / R_0\) against \(\varepsilon\). With a relative resistance change of about \num{0.38} at the full \SI{800}{\micro\strain} (\cref{tab:opt_results}), the optimized conductive-PLA element reaches a gauge factor of approximately \num{480}, far larger than the value of about \num{2} typical of metallic foil strain gauges~\cite{metal_gauge}. The two active arms of the assembled bridge in \cref{fig:finalWheatstoneSensorer} reach relative resistance changes of about \num{0.6} and \num{0.2}, corresponding to gauge factors of approximately \num{750} and \num{250}. This spread brackets the optimized single-element value and illustrates the print-to-print variability inherent to the deposition of the conductive traxel, the consequences of which for reproducibility and calibration are taken up in \cref{sec:Discussion}.
