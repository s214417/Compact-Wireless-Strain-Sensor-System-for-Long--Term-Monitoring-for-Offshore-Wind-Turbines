The purpose of this chapter is to present the developed system as a whole and thereby prepare the reader for the following chapters, which treat each part in detail.

The system consists of compact, battery-powered sensor modules intended for permanent installation on the bolted flange joints of an offshore wind turbine tower, together with a wireless network that collects the measurements and presents them to the user on a server that can be accessed onshore. 
The concept is illustrated in \cref{fig:System_concept}. Each module is mounted directly on one face of an M48 nut and combines 3D printed strain sensors, measurement and processing electronics, and a battery within an encapsulated footprint of $30 \times \SI{30}{\milli\meter}$. When the preload of the joint changes, the resulting surface strain of the nut is transferred to the sensor, whose resistance change is converted into a digital measurement and transmitted wirelessly.
One module is installed per monitored bolt, and the design is therefore driven by the need for a compact footprint, a unit cost low enough for deployment across the large number of bolted joints in a turbine, autonomous operation for more than ten years, and robustness to offshore environmental conditions.

The functional architecture of a single module and the surrounding infrastructure is shown in \cref{fig:System_blocks}. 
The 3D printed sensing elements are arranged in a Wheatstone bridge, which converts the strain-dependent change in resistance into a differential voltage. 
A microcontroller unit (MCU) periodically excites the bridge, samples this voltage with its internal analog-to-digital converter, and forms a digital measurement. The radio transceiver integrated in the MCU then passes the measurement to the Radio Frequency (RF) front-end, the antenna and its matching network, from where it is transmitted over a wireless network to the server that the operator accesses onshore.
To meet the ten-year lifetime requirement, the module spends nearly all of its time in a low-power sleep state and wakes only briefly to excite the bridge, take a measurement, and report it before returning to sleep. This low-power strategy governs the firmware design and forms the basis of the power budget of the system.

The remainder of the report follows this architecture. \Cref{sec:Sensor} covers the design and characterization of the 3D printed sensor, while \Cref{sec:Hardware} treats the selection of the wireless solution together with the schematic and PCB design of the module electronics. \Cref{sec:Firmware_and_software} describes the firmware and the user-facing software, and \Cref{sec:Results} presents the experimental validation of the integrated prototype. The system is developed as a proof of concept, and design decisions are accordingly evaluated against the requirements above rather than against full qualification for offshore deployment.
