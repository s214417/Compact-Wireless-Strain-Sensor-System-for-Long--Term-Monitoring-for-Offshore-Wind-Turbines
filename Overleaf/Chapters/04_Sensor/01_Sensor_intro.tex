As established in the system overview (\Cref{sec:System_overview}), each sensor node is mounted on the side face of an M48 nut, where the printed sensing elements are responsible for converting the deformation of the nut into a measurable electrical signal. This chapter develops and characterises that element, from its fabrication and mechanical structure through to the bridge configuration in which it is read out.

The sensor must turn the small surface strain of the nut into a resistance change that is both repeatable and large enough to be measured reliably, which is precisely the sub-question of accurate strain transfer and sensitivity posed in \cref{sec:ProblemStatement}. This behaviour must be preserved across the full deployment envelope, which spans an operating range of \SIrange{-20}{50}{\celsius} and a service life of more than ten years. The defining constraint is the magnitude of the measurand, since the system targets strains of at least \SI{800}{\micro\strain} across the \SI{30}{\milli\meter} nut face, a level more than an order of magnitude below the strains at which this type of printed sensor has previously been characterised \cite{G.O.A.T}. Obtaining usable sensitivity at this scale is the central difficulty treated in the remainder of the chapter.

The sensor is produced by fused deposition modelling (FDM), an additive process in which a thermoplastic filament is melted and deposited layer by layer to build a part directly from a digital model. Two materials are combined in a single build through multi-material printing, where a conductive PLA filament forms the sensing trace and a flexible thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU) substrate provides the compliant base onto which the trace is deposited. Producing it in this manner allows its geometry and material layout to be altered rapidly and at low cost, which supports the iterative build-and-test methodology used throughout the project and the wider aim of a sensor inexpensive enough to be deployed across the many bolted joints of a turbine.
 
The underlying sensing mechanism, together with the serpentine constriction-resistance sensor on which this work builds, is established in prior work \cite{G.O.A.T} and recalled in \cref{sec:Theory}, and is therefore not reproduced here. The contribution of the present chapter lies instead in optimising the print process for repeatability, in characterising the sensor under the small-strain, drift, and temperature conditions specific to this application, and in realising a temperature-compensated half-bridge suited to the nut-mounted geometry.
 
The sensing material was initially TPU-based, on the expectation that PLA would not withstand the low-temperature end of the operating range, an assumption that is revisited once the supporting measurements have been presented. The chapter first describes the sensor structure and its small-strain operating point, then the experimental setup used for characterisation, followed by the drift and temperature behaviour that informs the choice of sensing material, and closes with the design of the Wheatstone half-bridge.
