The microcontroller board (Arduino Uno, item~1 in \Cref{fig:setup}) is the central controller of the test bench, coordinating both the actuation and the measurement. It drives the digital stepper driver (item~2), which steps the stepper motor (item~3), then rotating the lead screw of the linear stage (item~4), and generating the linear travel that strains the device under test (DUT).
To synchronize the measurements with this motion, the microcontroller board sends a digital trigger to the Input/Output (I/O) port of the source meter (Keithley 2450, item~5), so that each measurement starts at a known motor position and time stamp. The DC power supply (item~6) powers the motor through the stepper driver and can additionally supply the excitation voltage for a Wheatstone bridge.

The DUT is mounted in a 3D-printed plastic fixture that couples it between the fixed frame and the moving carriage of the linear stage, so that the stage travel is transferred to the sensor as a controlled displacement. To allow each sensor to be fastened and released repeatedly, the fixture incorporates embedded nuts, threaded metal inserts set into the printed plastic, into which the mounting screws are driven. Printing the fixture in this way keeps it quick and inexpensive to produce and adapt, consistent with the iterative build-and-test approach used throughout the project.

The problem statement (\Cref{sec:ProblemStatement}) requires the sensor to be able to measure strains of at least \SI{800}{\micro\strain} over a square surface with a side length of \SI{30}{\milli\meter}. To produce this strain on the test bench, the required elongation follows from the definition of strain (\Cref{eq:strain definition}, \Cref{sec:Strain}):

\begin{align}\label{eq:microStrain}
    \Delta L = \varepsilon L_{0} = \SI{800}{\micro\strain} \cdot \SI{30}{\milli\meter} = \SI{24}{\micro\meter}.
\end{align}

Realizing such a small elongation slowly and with high resolution requires a fine actuation step. The microstepping of the digital stepper driver is therefore set to its maximum of \num{25600} pulses per revolution, which yields a microstep size of:

\begin{align} \label{eq:microstep}
    \text{microstep size} = \frac{\text{travel per revolution}}{\text{pulses per revolution}} = \frac{\SI{5}{\milli\meter}}{\num{25600}} = \SI{195.3}{\nano\meter}.
\end{align}

Reaching the target elongation of \SI{24}{\micro\meter} therefore requires \num{123} microsteps, the smallest integer above the exact value of \num{122.88}. Rounding up rather than down ensures that the target is reached rather than narrowly missed, giving an actual elongation of \SI{24.02}{\micro\meter}, corresponding to \SI{800.78}{\micro\strain}. The full strain range of at least \SI{800}{\micro\strain} required by the problem statement is therefore covered.
